A personal energy auto-biography #

By any reasonable definition, I’m not one of these “energetic aliens”. To prove it, here’s a list of Stephen’s personal limitations. I need >=7.5 hours of sleep to do hard thinking (e.g. math). I top out at 4-6 hours of coding straight even when I’m really engaged in what I’m doing. I’ve never consistently worked more than 50-60 hours of week. I can’t productively pull all-nighters without seriously degrading my focus, even when I really need to.

In fact, some of my biggest failures in life have come from tricking myself into thinking I could behave like an energetic alien. For example, in college, I’d often struggle with problem set classes because I’d procrastinate and then run out of stamina trying to brute force my way through them at the last minute.

If anything, realizing I’m not an energetic alien has helped me consciously compensate for my limitations. Over the years, I’ve found a bunch of strategies that seem to help me efficiently use and maximize my energy given what feel like fairly hard limitations:

  • Protect my mornings so that I can do focused work when I’m freshest.
  • Do my best, but often fail, to limit browsing the internet, since that diffuses my focus.
  • Loosely follow an intermittent fasting schedule because fasting helps me maintain a focused state.
  • Carefully plan to avoid having to sprint for last minute deadlines as much as possible.
  • Implement compensating strategies that assume I’ll make more mistakes the more I stretch my energetic limits.
  • Pick jobs & areas of focus which reward system-building and plodding over long periods of time rather than repeated short sprints.
  • Avoid committing to too many different activities, projects, etc.
  • Seek to increase depth of focus rather than length.

That said, I also don’t want to profess false humility or falsely victimize myself. Some people have it much worse and still overcome their limitations to do incredible work. As this article about Laura Hillenbrand discusses, she has a chronic fatigue impairment which makes it nearly impossible for her to keep to a consistent writing schedule, yet she manages to write popular, thorough, and fascinating books like Seabiscuit and Unbroken.
